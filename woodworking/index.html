Outside of work, one of my hobbies is woodworking. I particularly enjoy the contrast to my day-job and I find it an effective way to unwind while also channeling my passion for making.

I almost exclusively use hand-tools. This is mostly because I enjoy the process and the connection with the piece it builds, knowing that it was made purely from my own energy. Practical reasons also play a concern: I have nowhere to put a band saw, table saw, planer, router, mill or lathe.

The larger projects I have undertaken are listed here.

Finished: August 2018
Description: The under-stairs drawers were my first sizeable carpentry project. It took me a year start-to-finish, however it spent months at a time parked waiting for me to find enthusiasm to continue it - it probably would have taken about 10 consecutive weeks (of evenings and weekends).
I learned a lot doing it - most of all not to assume any surface is parallel or perpendicular to any other when dealing with house frames!
I started by knocking out the wall underneath my stairs, which was previously a very impractical cupboard accessible only from behind. In the resulting void I constructed a frame for the drawers to run in and built it into the studs I had revealed when I removed the wall. I ensured the frame had sufficient horizontal supports to attach the drawer runners to later.
Next I lined the frame with plywood to hide the frame when the drawers were extended. This was easier said than done - the drawer voids were all 1m deep and none of them were comfortable spaces for an adult. I then faced the front of the frame to bring it flush with the outer stringer. This mostly finished the frame.
For the drawers, I started by making the fronts out of oak. This was more time-consuming than it should have been as they were all different sizes, lacked right-angles (a result of tying the drawer frame to that of the house) and needed tapering to ensure a flush look when closed. Having made the fronts, I constructed the bodies out of 12mm plywood, butt-jointed, glued and screwed together. Not a fancy work of joinery, but they were large drawers and I knew I was going to paint them. These went together fairly quickly, and then received several coats of paint. They all have notches at the rear as the corridor they will extend into isn't actually wide enough to simply slide them in, so they have to be dropped and rotated in - which the notches allow (and turned out to be an interesting mathematical solution to calculate the correct size and position).
Finally I fitted the soft-close, push-to-open full-extension runners (the longest I could find at 700mm), using 3 per drawer for the triangular drawers and 4 for the rectangular one. This ensured they were strong enough to handle being fully loaded. To the same end I also reinforced the drawers with vertical bearers. The downside of using multiple runners per drawer is obviously that they must be aligned perfectly, and I spent 12-hours fine-tuning all the runners to ensure each drawer closed smoothly and the push-to-open worked (and had enough space behind the front to push into).
I'm really pleased with the result - I love how it looks, and it has massively increased how accessible that storage space is.

Finished: January 2019
Description: I made this blackboard as a birthday gift for my parents when they failed to find something of adequate quality which would fit in their kitchen - the best they could find was a poor quality blackboard from Amazon with some of the worst mitres I've ever seen (unforgivable when mass-produced by machine).
To make it I bought a cheap LUNS blackboard from IKEA, which I liked as chalk adhered to it well and it was magnetic. I then carefully extracted the blackboard surface (and turned the frame into kindling) and cut it to the desired size. Next I created a frame out of oak, joined with half-lap mites all around, ploughed a ⅛ groove in all of them and cut a key-hole mounting mechanism. Finally I added a shelf for chalk before finishing with shellac.
The entire project took me two weeks.

Desk

  • gallery/desk_1.jpg
Image 0 of 0 ← →
Finished: May 2018
Description: My desk is made from 4 solid oak boards, laminated together. Each board was 225 x 38 mm and 1.8m long. The desk is 1.7m wide, 87cm deep and 74cm high. Being solid oak it is exceptionally solid. I contracted a steel-works in Cornwall to make the legs, which are standard 80 x 40 mm box-section steel welded into a rectangle and simply clear-coated to preserve their industrial appearance. The top is finished in Polyurethane to stand up to daily use.
The entire project only took me 1 weekend, plus evenings of the subsequent week to apply finish. I'm really happy with how it turned out and I ensured I placed the most figured board at the front to give the desk character.

Finished: April 2018
Description: Made from 15, 4m long red-wood 2x4s this was my first large project. It was made to a Paul Sellers design, modified to measure 5ft x 3ft, with a well in the center. It also has two shelves between the legs to add storage and even more weight (it weighs 130kg unladen). It's outfitted with a 10½-inch quick-release vice, mounted on the left-hand end to make the most of the space in the workshop (despite me being right-handed).
I really enjoyed making this - pine is a lovely material to work with, and converting a stack of 2x4s into a large piece of furniture was exceptionally rewarding. It was a particularly challenging project as I did it just using a Black & Decker workmate.
The project took me 5 months start to finish, although there were significant pauses during that time. I also had to start-over as it emerged the first batch of 2x4s were far too warped to be useable and couldn't be laminated straight. Cutting my losses and restarting was gutting, but the right call.
